The liberty of communication cannot be mine till       it has lost all its value!”        She was now, on being settled at home, at leisure to observe the       real state of her sister’s spirits. Jane was not happy. She still       cherished a very tender affection for Bingley. Having never even       fancied herself in love before, her regard had all the warmth of       first attachment, and, from her age and disposition, greater       steadiness than most first attachments often boast; and so       fervently did she value his remembrance, and prefer him to every       other man, that all her good sense, and all her attention to the       feelings of her friends, were requisite to check the indulgence       of those regrets which must have been injurious to her own health       and their tranquillity.        “Well, Lizzy,” said Mrs. Bennet one day, “what is your opinion       _now_ of this sad business of Jane’s? For my part, I am       determined never to speak of it again to anybody. I told my       sister Phillips so the other day. But I cannot find out that Jane       saw anything of him in London.
