But I pity her, because       she must feel that she has been acting wrong, and because I am       very sure that anxiety for her brother is the cause of it. I need       not explain myself farther; and though _we_ know this anxiety to       be quite needless, yet if she feels it, it will easily account       for her behaviour to me; and so deservedly dear as he is to his       sister, whatever anxiety she must feel on his behalf is natural       and amiable. I cannot but wonder, however, at her having any such       fears now, because, if he had at all cared about me, we must have       met, long ago. He knows of my being in town, I am certain, from       something she said herself; and yet it would seem, by her manner       of talking, as if she wanted to persuade herself that he is       really partial to Miss Darcy. I cannot understand it. If I were       not afraid of judging harshly, I should be almost tempted to say       that there is a strong appearance of duplicity in all this.
