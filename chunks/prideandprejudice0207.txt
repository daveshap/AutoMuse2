She will make him a very proper wife.”        Mr. Collins and Charlotte were both standing at the gate in       conversation with the ladies; and Sir William, to Elizabeth’s       high diversion, was stationed in the doorway, in earnest       contemplation of the greatness before him, and constantly bowing       whenever Miss de Bourgh looked that way.        At length there was nothing more to be said; the ladies drove on,       and the others returned into the house. Mr. Collins no sooner saw       the two girls than he began to congratulate them on their good       fortune, which Charlotte explained by letting them know that the       whole party was asked to dine at Rosings the next day.     Chapter 29        Mr. Collins’s triumph, in consequence of this invitation, was       complete. The power of displaying the grandeur of his patroness       to his wondering visitors, and of letting them see her civility       towards himself and his wife, was exactly what he had wished for;       and that an opportunity of doing it should be given so soon, was       such an instance of Lady Catherine’s condescension, as he knew       not how to admire enough.        “I confess,” said he, “that I should not have been at all       surprised by her ladyship’s asking us on Sunday to drink tea and       spend the evening at Rosings.
