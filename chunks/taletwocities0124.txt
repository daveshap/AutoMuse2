Yes, I am very much put out.”  “May I ask the cause?”  “I don’t want dozens of people who are not at all worthy of Ladybird, to come here looking after her,” said Miss Pross.  “_Do_ dozens come for that purpose?”  “Hundreds,” said Miss Pross.  It was characteristic of this lady (as of some other people before her time and since) that whenever her original proposition was questioned, she exaggerated it.  “Dear me!” said Mr. Lorry, as the safest remark he could think of.  “I have lived with the darling--or the darling has lived with me, and paid me for it; which she certainly should never have done, you may take your affidavit, if I could have afforded to keep either myself or her for nothing--since she was ten years old. And it’s really very hard,”  said Miss Pross.  Not seeing with precision what was very hard, Mr. Lorry shook his head; using that important part of himself as a sort of fairy cloak that would fit anything.  “All sorts of people who are not in the least degree worthy of the pet, are always turning up,” said Miss Pross. “When you began it--”  “_I_ began it, Miss Pross?”  “Didn’t you? Who brought her father to life?”  “Oh! If _that_ was beginning it--” said Mr. Lorry.  “It wasn’t ending it, I suppose?
