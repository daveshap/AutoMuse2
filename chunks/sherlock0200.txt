Our footfalls rang out crisply and loudly as we swung through the doctors’ quarter, Wimpole Street, Harley Street, and so through Wigmore Street into Oxford Street. In a quarter of an hour we were in Bloomsbury at the Alpha Inn, which is a small public-house at the corner of one of the streets which runs down into Holborn. Holmes pushed open the door of the private bar and ordered two glasses of beer from the ruddy-faced, white-aproned landlord.  “Your beer should be excellent if it is as good as your geese,” said he.  “My geese!” The man seemed surprised.  “Yes. I was speaking only half an hour ago to Mr. Henry Baker, who was a member of your goose club.”  “Ah! yes, I see. But you see, sir, them’s not _our_ geese.”  “Indeed! Whose, then?”  “Well, I got the two dozen from a salesman in Covent Garden.”  “Indeed? I know some of them. Which was it?”  “Breckinridge is his name.”  “Ah! I don’t know him. Well, here’s your good health landlord, and prosperity to your house. Good-night.”  “Now for Mr. Breckinridge,” he continued, buttoning up his coat as we came out into the frosty air. “Remember, Watson that though we have so homely a thing as a goose at one end of this chain, we have at the other a man who will certainly get seven years’ penal servitude unless we can establish his innocence.
